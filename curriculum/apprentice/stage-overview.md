# Apprentice Stage Overview
*Ages 13-15*

## Philosophy

The Apprentice stage marks the transition from learning about the world to working in it. A thirteen-year-old is not a large child — they are a young adult with real capacity for real contribution. Historically, people this age were apprenticed to blacksmiths, ran family farms, sailed ships, and built businesses. The modern instinct to keep teenagers in a holding pattern of schoolwork and extracurriculars wastes the most energetic, idealistic years of their lives.

In the Codex framework, the Apprentice stage introduces specialization and real-world scope. The student chooses two or three pillars to focus on deeply and begins producing work that has value beyond the classroom. They write code that someone uses. They build things that someone needs. They grow food that someone eats. They create content that someone reads. The feedback loop shifts from "the parent says good job" to "the world responds." That response — a customer who pays, a user who complains, a garden that fails, a structure that holds — is the most powerful teacher available.

Mentorship is the defining relationship of this stage. The parent remains present but is no longer the primary teacher in most domains. The student needs adults who do the thing they want to learn — a carpenter, a programmer, a farmer, a business owner, a writer, a coach — and who will let the student work alongside them, make mistakes in a real context, and gradually take on more responsibility. Finding, securing, and maintaining these mentorship relationships is itself a skill the student must develop.

## Readiness Indicators

- Has demonstrated sustained commitment to at least one domain across multiple projects (Builder stage)
- Can work independently for 2+ hours on a challenging task
- Shows desire to do "real" work, not exercises — expresses frustration with artificial assignments
- Can communicate effectively with adults outside the family (teachers, coaches, shop owners)
- Has basic competency in core tools of at least one domain (hand tools, programming language, cooking equipment, etc.)
- Demonstrates self-awareness about strengths and weaknesses — can articulate what they are good at and what they want to improve
- Is physically and emotionally ready for increased autonomy — can handle unsupervised work time responsibly

## Content Approach

Apprentice content is written directly to the student. The voice is that of a senior colleague, not a teacher — matter-of-fact, occasionally blunt, always respectful. We assume the student is capable and motivated. We do not over-explain. We do not hedge with excessive caveats. We present the standard, the method, and the challenge, and we trust them to rise to it.

Content should be:
- **Real-world scoped** — projects have real users, real stakeholders, or real consequences
- **Specialization-oriented** — depth over breadth; mastery over exposure
- **Mentor-dependent** — many units require or strongly recommend working with a skilled adult outside the family
- **Multi-week** — major projects span weeks or months, requiring sustained commitment and time management
- **Portfolio-building** — every major project produces a tangible artifact (built object, deployed code, written work, documented process) that demonstrates competency
- **Failure-rich** — content explicitly addresses what goes wrong and treats recovery as the learning moment
- **Increasingly independent** — the parent-role shifts from `facilitate` to `observe` to `absent` across this stage

Safety levels trend toward `yellow` and occasionally `red` — the student is using real tools, real heat, real height, real code in production. Supervision shifts from constant to periodic check-in.

## Pillar Focus at This Stage

### Agency & Critical Thinking
Formal logic and rhetoric. Evaluating complex arguments with multiple valid perspectives. Independent research on controversial topics. Making and defending decisions with real consequences. Understanding cognitive biases in themselves and others. Developing a personal epistemology — how do I decide what is true?

### American Dynamism
Deep study of American systems: how government actually works, how markets actually work, how communities actually function. Entrepreneurship as a practical skill, not an abstraction. The student starts or contributes to a real venture — a lawn care business, an online shop, a freelance skill. Understanding civic responsibility through direct participation.

### Building & Engineering
Specialization in a trade or engineering discipline. Woodworking: joinery, finishing, complex construction. Metalworking introduction. Electrical wiring and basic electronics. Design and prototyping. The student should be able to build something that an adult would pay for. Introduction to CAD and digital fabrication if accessible.

### Character & Purpose
Identity formation — who am I becoming and who do I want to be? Grappling with ethical complexity (not just "lying is wrong" but "when is honesty cruel?"). Service at community scale. Leadership of younger children. Study of historical and philosophical ideas about virtue, duty, and meaning. Faith exploration or deepening.

### Core Academics
Self-directed academic work. The student reads primary sources, not just textbooks. Writing is persuasive, analytical, and creative — essays, proposals, stories. Mathematics is applied to real problems: statistics from their own data, geometry in their builds, algebra in their code. Science is investigative — the student designs and executes original experiments.

### Food & Farming
Management of a full food system: garden planning, growing, harvesting, preserving, cooking. The student can feed the family independently. Advanced cooking techniques. Understanding agricultural economics. Optional: small-scale animal husbandry (chickens, bees). Food as a lens for understanding supply chains, economics, and environmental impact.

### Physical & Survival
Wilderness leadership — the student can plan and lead a multi-day expedition. Advanced survival skills: water sourcing and purification, shelter in adverse conditions, navigation in unfamiliar terrain. Physical training shifts to sport-specific or strength-building. The student understands their body well enough to train intelligently and recover properly.

### Software & AI
Building real applications with real users. Full-stack web development. Understanding databases, APIs, and deployment. Contributing to open-source projects. Using AI as a tool while understanding its limitations. The student can build something useful, deploy it, and iterate based on user feedback. Introduction to cybersecurity and digital privacy.

## Example Progressions from Builder Stage

- **Builder: Build a bookshelf** becomes **Apprentice: Build custom furniture with proper joinery for a paying client** — from functional to professional quality
- **Builder: Python fundamentals** becomes **Apprentice: Build and deploy a web application that solves a real problem** — from scripts to products
- **Builder: Full season garden** becomes **Apprentice: Manage a market garden and sell produce** — from growing for family to growing for customers
- **Builder: Research report** becomes **Apprentice: Independent research on a complex topic, producing a published article or presentation** — from school-format to real-format
- **Builder: Multi-day camping trip** becomes **Apprentice: Plan and lead a wilderness expedition for a small group** — from participant to leader
- **Builder: Cook dinner for the family** becomes **Apprentice: Plan a week of meals, manage the grocery budget, and execute daily** — from single meals to food systems

## Content Needed

### Agency & Critical Thinking
- [ ] Formal logic — identifying valid and invalid arguments, understanding logical structure
- [ ] Cognitive bias workshop — recognizing biases in yourself and in media/advertising
- [ ] Independent research project — choose a controversial topic, research multiple perspectives, present findings
- [ ] Negotiation and persuasion — real scenarios requiring the student to advocate for themselves
- [ ] Personal decision framework — building a system for making high-stakes decisions

### American Dynamism
- [ ] Start a micro-business — from idea through first paying customer, with real accounting
- [ ] How government actually works — attend a city council meeting, follow a bill, understand the machinery
- [ ] Market economics lab — study supply and demand using a real local market (farmers market, flea market)
- [ ] Civic engagement project — identify a local issue, research solutions, present to a decision-maker
- [ ] American innovation deep dive — original research on one American innovation and its full chain of impact

### Building & Engineering
- [ ] Joinery mastery — mortise and tenon, dovetail, dado: build a project using at least two joint types
- [ ] Electrical wiring basics — wire a circuit, install a switch, understand residential wiring safely
- [ ] Design and prototype — take a product idea from sketch to CAD to physical prototype
- [ ] Build for a client — complete a building project to someone else's specifications and standards
- [ ] Metalworking introduction — basic welding or blacksmithing with a mentor

### Character & Purpose
- [ ] Ethical complexity discussions — a series of real dilemmas with no clean answers and competing goods
- [ ] Leadership practicum — lead a group of younger students through a Builder-stage project
- [ ] Personal philosophy statement — what I believe and why, written and defended
- [ ] Community service at scale — organize and execute a service project involving multiple people
- [ ] Study a virtue tradition — Stoicism, a faith tradition, bushido, or another ethical framework in depth

### Core Academics
- [ ] Primary source analysis — read and analyze original documents, not summaries
- [ ] Persuasive essay series — argue positions on real issues with evidence and structure
- [ ] Statistical analysis project — collect original data, calculate statistics, draw conclusions
- [ ] Independent reading program — 12 books per year from a curated list, with written reflections
- [ ] Original scientific investigation — design an experiment, collect data, present results

### Food & Farming
- [ ] Market garden project — grow produce for sale, manage costs and revenue
- [ ] Advanced cooking techniques — knife skills, sauce making, bread, pastry, fermentation
- [ ] Food preservation at scale — canning, smoking, fermting, and root cellaring
- [ ] Meal planning and budget management — feed the family for a week on a fixed budget
- [ ] Animal husbandry introduction — chickens, bees, or rabbits with a mentor

### Physical & Survival
- [ ] Expedition leadership — plan and lead a 3-5 day wilderness trip for a small group
- [ ] Advanced navigation — off-trail navigation using map, compass, and terrain association
- [ ] Personal training program — design a 12-week fitness program based on personal goals
- [ ] Wilderness first aid — practical first aid skills for remote settings
- [ ] Adverse weather survival — shelter, fire, and water management in rain, cold, and heat

### Software & AI
- [ ] Full-stack web application — build a complete app with frontend, backend, database, and deployment
- [ ] Open-source contribution — find a project, understand the codebase, submit a meaningful pull request
- [ ] AI tools and critical thinking — use AI assistants productively while identifying their failures
- [ ] Cybersecurity fundamentals — understanding threats, encryption, password management, and privacy
- [ ] Build a tool for someone else — identify a real person's real problem and build software that solves it
