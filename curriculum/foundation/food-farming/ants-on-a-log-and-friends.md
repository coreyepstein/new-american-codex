# Ants on a Log and Friends

## Overview

This is the first recipe. It's not about producing a perfect snack — it's about a small person standing at a counter, touching real food, following steps in order, and eating something they made with their own hands. Ants on a Log (celery + peanut butter + raisins) is the perfect starter recipe: no heat, no sharp knives, three ingredients, and a result that looks fun and tastes good.

## The Learning

Cooking is sequencing, measurement, chemistry, patience, and creativity — all disguised as making something delicious. At the foundation stage, the learning is simpler:

- **Sequencing:** First the celery, then the spread, then the toppings. Order matters.
- **Fine motor skills:** Spreading, placing raisins one at a time, pouring.
- **Vocabulary:** Celery, raisin, spread, scoop, place. Name everything out loud.
- **Ownership:** "I made this." That sentence changes a child's relationship with food.

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Celery | 3-4 stalks | Washed and cut into 4-inch pieces by an adult |
| Peanut butter (or alternative) | 3-4 tablespoons | Sunflower seed butter for nut-free; cream cheese also works |
| Raisins | Small handful | Dried cranberries or chocolate chips as alternatives |
| Apple slices | 2-3 (optional) | Can substitute for celery for kids who dislike celery |
| Banana | Half (optional) | Sliced into rounds — another "log" option |

## Equipment

- Child-safe butter knife or small spoon
- Plate or cutting board (use a damp towel underneath to keep it from sliding)
- Small bowls for ingredients (easier for little hands than jars)

## Instructions

### Prep (5 minutes — adult does this)

1. Wash and cut celery into 4-inch pieces. Cut a thin slice off the rounded back so each piece sits flat without rocking.
2. Scoop peanut butter (or alternative) into a small bowl.
3. Put raisins in another small bowl.
4. Set everything on the table or counter at your child's height. Use a step stool if needed.
5. Wash hands together. Make it part of the ritual: *"Chefs always wash their hands before cooking."*

### Cook (7 minutes — child does this with you)

**Step 1: Pick your log.**
*"Choose a piece of celery and put it on your plate. This is the log!"*

**Step 2: Spread the peanut butter.**
*"Scoop a little peanut butter with your knife. Now spread it inside the celery. Fill up the little ditch!"*

Demonstrate on your own piece first. Then let them try. It will be messy. That's correct.

If spreading is too hard for younger children (18-24 months), pre-spread the peanut butter and let them do Step 3.

**Step 3: Add the ants.**
*"Now put the raisins on top. These are the ants walking on the log!"*

Let them place raisins one at a time. Count together as they go: *"One ant, two ants, three ants..."*

**Step 4: Make more!**
Repeat with remaining celery. Encourage variations: *"Want to try this one with cream cheese? Or extra ants?"*

### Serve (3 minutes)

Arrange the finished logs on the plate. Let your child decide the arrangement.

*"You made this! Would you like to share one with someone, or eat them all yourself?"*

Eat together. Talk about the textures and flavors: *"Is the celery crunchy or soft? Are the raisins sweet or sour?"*

## The Science

Why does peanut butter stick to the celery?

*For your child:* *"Peanut butter is sticky! It grabs onto the celery and holds on tight. That's why the ants don't fall off."*

*For you:* Peanut butter's viscosity and fat content create adhesion to the celery's rough surface. It's a simple lesson in material properties — smooth things slide, sticky things hold. You can demonstrate: try putting raisins directly on celery without peanut butter. They fall off. The peanut butter is the "glue."

## Kitchen Skills Practiced

| Skill | How |
|-------|-----|
| Spreading | Using a knife or spoon to spread peanut butter |
| Scooping | Getting peanut butter from bowl to celery |
| Placing | Positioning raisins with fingers (pincer grasp) |
| Counting | Counting toppings as they go on |
| Sequencing | Following the 3-step order: log, spread, ants |

## Variations

- **Fruit log:** Use banana halves instead of celery. Spread with peanut butter and top with granola or coconut flakes.
- **Apple nachos:** Spread apple slices with nut butter and top with raisins and a drizzle of honey (over age 1 only for honey).
- **Cracker stacks:** Spread crackers with cream cheese, stack two together. Fancy cracker sandwiches.
- **Rainbow ants:** Use a mix of toppings — raisins, dried cranberries, sunflower seeds, chocolate chips — and let your child create a color pattern on the log.

## Discussion While You Cook

- *"Where does celery come from? It grows in the ground! A farmer grew this."*
- *"What are raisins? They're grapes that got dried in the sun! They used to be big and juicy."*
- *"Peanut butter is made from peanuts. Someone had to smash them all up!"*
- *"What's your favorite part — the crunchy part, the sticky part, or the sweet part?"*

## Safety Notes

- **Allergens:** Peanut butter is a major allergen. Know your child's allergy status before using. Sunflower seed butter or cream cheese are safe alternatives. If preparing for a group, always check with all parents.
- **Choking hazards:** Whole raisins and large pieces of celery can be choking hazards for children under 2. For younger children, chop raisins in half and make sure celery is cut into manageable sizes. Always supervise eating.
- **Butter knife safety:** Even "child-safe" knives have edges. Show your child how to hold it safely and keep fingers away from the spreading side. Stay within arm's reach.
- **Hygiene:** Wash hands before and after. If your child licks the knife, give them a fresh one — no double-dipping into shared bowls.
