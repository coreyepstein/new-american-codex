# Planting Seeds and Watching Them Grow

## Overview

Every tomato in the grocery store started as a seed in dirt. That fact is genuinely astonishing when you're three years old. This activity takes a tiny, hard, unremarkable seed and turns it into a living, growing plant — in your child's own hands, on your own windowsill. It's slow magic, and it teaches patience, responsibility, and the foundational truth that food comes from the earth.

## Setup

1. **Choose your seeds.** Bean seeds (lima, kidney, or green bean) are the best starter seeds — they're large enough for small fingers, they sprout fast (3-5 days), and the seedlings are dramatic. Sunflower seeds are also excellent. Radish seeds are tiny but sprout within 3 days.

2. **Prepare containers.** Poke 2-3 small drainage holes in the bottom of each cup or container. If using a clear plastic cup, you'll be able to see roots grow along the sides — this is incredibly compelling for young children.

3. **Set up a workspace.** Newspaper or a tray on the table. This will get dirty. That's the point.

## Instructions

### Step 1: Touch the Seed (3 minutes)

Before planting, let your child hold the seed. Really look at it.

*"This tiny thing is going to turn into a plant! All the instructions for how to grow are inside this little seed. It's like a baby plant, sleeping."*

Let them feel how hard it is. Compare it to the size of their fingernail. If you have a lima bean, you can gently split one open (soak it for a few hours first) and show the tiny embryo inside: *"See? That's the baby plant, curled up inside."*

### Step 2: Fill with Soil (5 minutes)

Let your child scoop soil into the container with their hands or a small cup. Fill it about 3/4 full.

*"This is the soil — it's the bed for the seed. It has food and water inside that the plant will eat."*

Let them squeeze the soil. Smell it. Get their hands fully in it. Sensory engagement is the lesson at this age as much as the botany.

### Step 3: Plant the Seed (3 minutes)

*"Poke a hole in the soil with your finger. Not too deep — just to your first knuckle."*

Show them on your own pot first. Then let them poke their hole.

*"Now drop the seed in the hole. One seed per hole."*

*"Cover it up with soil, like tucking it into bed."*

Pat the soil gently over the seed.

### Step 4: Water (3 minutes)

*"Now the seed needs a drink. Not too much — just a little, like the seed is having a small cup of water, not a bath."*

Use a spray bottle (easier for small hands to control) or a very small cup. The soil should be moist, not flooded.

### Step 5: Find a Home (2 minutes)

*"Where should our plant live? It needs sunshine. Where is it sunny in our house?"*

Walk around together and let your child choose the windowsill or sunny spot. Place the pot there.

### Step 6: Make a Label (Optional, 5 minutes)

Write the plant's name (or let your child draw a picture of what they think it will look like) on a popsicle stick. Press it into the soil.

*"This is so we remember what we planted!"*

### Step 7: The Daily Ritual (Ongoing, 5 minutes/day)

Every morning (or after breakfast — attach it to an existing routine):

*"Let's check on our plant! Does the soil feel dry?"*

Touch the soil together. If it's dry, water it. If it's moist, skip.

*"Do you see anything new?"*

For the first 3-5 days, nothing visible will happen. This is the patience lesson. Say: *"The seed is working hard under the soil. We can't see it yet, but it's growing."*

When the first sprout appears — and it will — treat it like an event: *"LOOK! It came up! The seed woke up and started growing!"*

## What to Watch For

- **Wonder at the sprout:** The moment they see the first green shoot breaking through is unforgettable. Take a photo. Let them tell someone about it.
- **Daily ownership:** Does your child remember to check the plant without being reminded? That's responsibility developing.
- **Questions:** "Why is it growing sideways?" "Why is this one taller?" "Can I eat it?" All excellent questions. Explore them together.
- **Gentle hands:** Learning to touch a seedling without breaking it is a real skill. If they snap one, don't scold — plant another. Say: *"Seedlings are fragile. Let's be extra gentle with the next one."*

## Variations

- **Clear cup method:** Plant in a clear plastic cup and watch the roots grow down the sides. This reveals the hidden half of the plant — the part underground that most people never think about.
- **Wet paper towel method:** Place bean seeds between wet paper towels in a ziplock bag. Tape to a window. You can watch the entire sprouting process without soil. Great for the very first planting experience.
- **Grow a snack:** Plant cherry tomato seedlings, snap pea seeds, or strawberry plants. When they produce food your child can eat, the farm-to-table connection becomes real.
- **Name the plant:** Let your child name their plant. Naming creates attachment, and attachment creates care.
- **Plant journal:** For 3-4-year-olds, draw a picture of the plant each week. Compare the drawings. *"Look how much it grew!"*

## Reflection Prompts

- *"What did the seed need to grow?"* (Water, sun, soil)
- *"How long did we wait before we saw anything?"*
- *"Is our plant alive? How do you know?"*
- *"Where does the food at the grocery store start?"* (As a seed, in the ground, on a farm)
- *"What do you want to plant next?"*

## Safety Notes

- **Soil ingestion:** Potting soil is not food-safe. Young children (especially under 2) will try to eat it. Supervise closely. Wash hands after handling soil.
- **Seeds as choking hazards:** Seeds are small and hard. Do not let young children put them in their mouth. Count the seeds you give them and account for all of them.
- **Water mess:** Overwatering will happen. Put pots on a tray or plate to catch overflow. Accept that the windowsill will get dirty.
- **Mold:** If soil stays too wet and mold appears, move the pot to better airflow and reduce watering. If mold is significant, start over with fresh soil — don't let a child handle moldy soil.
- **Allergies:** If using bean seeds, be aware of any legume allergies. Sunflower seeds are generally safe but check for seed allergies.
