# Counting With Real Objects

## Overview

Counting is not a worksheet skill. For a young child, counting is a *physical* act — touching a stone, dropping it into a bowl, hearing it clink. The number word "three" means nothing until they have held three acorns in their hand, placed them one by one into a basket, and felt the difference between three and five.

This lesson builds that foundation. No flashcards, no screens, no pressure to perform. Just you and your child, counting real things.

## Background for Parents

Young children learn to count in stages, and the stages overlap:

1. **Reciting** — They can say "one, two, three" like a song, but the words have no meaning yet. This is fine. The song comes first.
2. **One-to-one correspondence** — They touch one object per number word. This is a huge cognitive leap. Many children reach this around age 2-3.
3. **Cardinality** — They understand that the *last* number they say is how many there are. "One, two, three — there are THREE." This usually clicks between 3-4.
4. **Conservation** — They understand that spreading out five stones does not make "more" than five stones in a pile. This comes later, often 4-5+.

You are not trying to push them through these stages. You are giving them the raw material — real things to count — and trusting their brain to do its work.

## Lesson Flow

### Opening (2 minutes)

Spread the blanket or towel on the floor. Dump out your collection of objects. Let your child explore them — pick them up, feel their weight, stack them, line them up. This free-play time is not wasted. It is essential. They need to *know* the objects before they can count them.

Say something like: *"Look at all these stones! Let's see how many we have."*

### Core (5 minutes)

**For ages 1-2: The Drop-and-Count Game**

Hold the bowl in front of your child. Pick up one stone. Say *"One!"* with enthusiasm and drop it in. The clink matters — it is auditory feedback.

Hand your child a stone: *"Your turn! Drop it in."*

When they drop it, say *"Two!"*

Keep going until they lose interest. Three is plenty. Five is a win. Ten is a miracle. Follow their lead.

**For ages 2-3: Touch-and-Count**

Line up 3-5 stones in a row. Touch each one as you count together: *"One... two... three."*

Then: *"How many stones? Three!"* (Point to the whole group.)

Let them try. Their finger will probably skip stones or count the same one twice. That is completely normal. Do not correct. Just model it again, slowly.

**For ages 3-4: Sort-and-Count**

Mix two types of objects — three stones and two acorns. *"Can you put the stones in this bowl and the acorns in that bowl?"*

After sorting: *"How many stones? Let's count. How many acorns? Which bowl has more?"*

### Practice (2 minutes)

Put all the objects back in the main pile. Ask: *"Can you give me three stones?"* (Hold out your hand.)

If they hand you a random handful, count what they gave you together. *"Let's see — one, two, three, four, five! You gave me five! That's even more than three."*

No wrong answers. Just counting.

### Closing (1 minute)

*"Let's put them all away together. One... two... three..."* Count each object as it goes back in the bag or basket. Cleanup is a counting opportunity.

## Assessment

You are not grading a toddler. You are noticing. Over days and weeks, watch for:

- Do they recite number words in order, even if imperfectly? ("One, two, free, five" — the sequence is forming)
- When they touch objects, do they move in a roughly one-per-word rhythm?
- Do they start counting things on their own — steps, crackers, dogs at the park?
- When you ask "how many?", do they repeat the last number, or just start counting again?

All of these are progress. Write nothing down. Just watch.

## Adaptations

- **Non-verbal children:** Count with hand-over-hand guidance. Touch each object together. The physical rhythm matters more than saying the words.
- **Highly active children:** Count jumps, stomps, or claps instead of sitting with objects. *"How many times can you jump? One! Two! Three!"*
- **Children who resist structured activity:** Count incidentally — stairs on the way up, blueberries going onto the plate, socks coming out of the dryer.

## Going Deeper

- **Counting books** — *Ten Black Dots* by Donald Crews, *Chicka Chicka 1, 2, 3* by Bill Martin Jr. Read them often. Point to the objects on each page and count together.
- **Nature counting** — On walks, count pinecones, flowers, birds, puddles. The world is full of things to count.
- **Cooking** — *"We need three eggs. Can you help me count? One... two... three."* Real tasks with real stakes (the recipe needs exactly three).
- **Body counting** — *"How many fingers? How many toes? How many eyes? How many noses?"* They never get tired of this one.
