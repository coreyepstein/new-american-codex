# The Family Garden

## Overview

A garden is the most honest teacher a child will ever have. It doesn't grade on a curve. It doesn't give partial credit. If you water too much, the roots rot. If you water too little, the plant wilts. If you plant in the wrong season, nothing comes up. And when you get it right — when your child pulls a carrot out of the ground that they planted as a seed eight weeks ago — something shifts in their understanding of where food comes from and what it takes to produce it. This project spans an entire growing cycle.

## The Deliverable

A functioning food garden that produces at least one harvest your family eats. Along the way: a garden journal documenting the process from planning through harvest, and the lived experience of tending something alive over weeks.

## Materials & Tools

See the frontmatter list. If you don't have yard space, containers on a patio, balcony, or sunny porch work well. Even a single large pot growing cherry tomatoes or herbs counts. The scale doesn't matter — the process does.

**Beginner-friendly crops (fast, forgiving, rewarding):**
- Radishes (harvest in 25-30 days — the fastest gratification)
- Lettuce and salad greens (harvest leaves in 30-45 days)
- Bush beans (harvest in 50-60 days)
- Cherry tomatoes (from starter plants, harvest in 60-80 days)
- Herbs: basil, cilantro, chives (continuous harvest)
- Sunflowers (not food, but dramatic and motivating — harvest seeds in 70-80 days)

Check your local planting zone and last frost date. Your local garden center or county extension office can tell you what to plant when.

## Project Phases

### Phase 1: Plan the Garden (Session 1, 30 minutes)

**Choose the location (10 minutes):**

Walk your yard or outdoor space together. You're looking for:
- **Sun:** At least 6 hours of direct sunlight per day (watch the spot throughout a day to confirm)
- **Water access:** Can you easily get a hose or watering can there?
- **Drainage:** Does water pool after rain? Avoid low spots.
- **Proximity:** A garden close to the house gets more attention than one in a far corner.

*"Plants need sun to grow — just like in our experiment. Let's find the sunniest spot."*

**Design on paper (10 minutes):**

In the garden journal, draw the garden plot. Decide what to plant where.

Rules of thumb for placement:
- Tall plants (tomatoes, sunflowers) on the north side so they don't shade shorter plants
- Group plants with similar water needs together
- Leave enough space between plants (seed packets tell you how much)

*"This is our garden blueprint. Real farmers plan before they plant. Let's figure out what goes where."*

**Choose your crops (10 minutes):**

Visit a garden center or browse seed packets online. Let your child choose at least one crop — even if you know it's not ideal. Ownership matters more than optimization for a first garden.

*"Pick something YOU want to grow. Something you'd be excited to eat."*

### Phase 2: Prepare and Plant (Session 2, 45 minutes)

**Prepare the soil (15 minutes):**

If using a new bed or containers, fill with a mix of garden soil and compost. If using an existing bed, loosen the soil with a garden fork and mix in compost.

Your child helps: scooping soil, mixing compost, raking the surface smooth. This is physical work and should feel that way.

*"Good soil is the most important thing in a garden. It's where plants get their food. See how dark this compost is? That's full of nutrients."*

**Plant (20 minutes):**

Follow seed packet instructions for depth and spacing. For each seed:
1. Your child pokes a hole to the right depth (use a finger or a pencil)
2. Drops in the seed
3. Covers gently
4. Waters lightly

For starter plants (tomatoes, herbs): dig a hole twice the size of the pot, place the plant, fill around it, water thoroughly.

**Label everything (10 minutes):**

Write the plant name and planting date on popsicle sticks. Push them into the soil next to each crop.

*"We need to remember what's where. In 3 weeks, all of these will look like little green sprouts and we won't be able to tell them apart without labels."*

Take a "Day 1" photo. Your child draws "Day 1" in the garden journal.

### Phase 3: Tend (Sessions 3-4, ongoing for weeks — 10 minutes daily, one longer session per week)

**Daily routine (your child's jobs):**
- Check soil moisture (stick a finger 1 inch into the soil — if dry, water; if moist, skip)
- Water with a gentle spray (not a hard blast that displaces seeds or soil)
- Look for changes and report: *"Anything different today?"*
- Pull any obvious weeds (teach the difference: *"Weeds are plants we didn't plant. If it's not in our plan, it's a weed."*)

**Weekly routine (15-20 minutes, do together):**
- Measure growth and record in the journal
- Draw or photograph the garden
- Check for pests (holes in leaves, visible insects)
- Add mulch if soil is drying out quickly
- Thin seedlings if they're overcrowded (this is hard — pulling out healthy seedlings feels wrong, but it's necessary)

*"Gardening isn't just planting and waiting. It's checking in every day. The garden depends on you."*

**When things go wrong (and they will):**
- Seeds that don't sprout: *"Not every seed makes it. That's why we plant extras."*
- Pests eating leaves: *"Those bugs are hungry too. Let's figure out what they are and whether we need to do something about them."*
- Wilting: *"It's telling us something. What do you think it needs?"*

Every problem is a lesson. Resist the urge to fix things silently — let your child observe, diagnose, and participate in the solution.

### Phase 4: Harvest (Session 5, 30 minutes)

The first harvest day is a celebration. When the first radish is ready, or the first tomato turns red, or the first lettuce leaf is big enough to eat:

*"You grew this. From a tiny seed, with water and sun and your care, this food exists because of you."*

Let your child pull, pick, or cut the harvest. Wash it. Eat it right there in the garden if you want — a sun-warm cherry tomato, a radish with dirt still on it (brush it off first), a leaf of basil.

**Cook with the harvest:** Even a small harvest can contribute to a meal. A handful of herbs in scrambled eggs. Lettuce in a salad. Radishes with butter and salt. Cherry tomatoes in pasta.

Record the harvest in the journal: what, how much, how it tasted, what you'd do differently.

## Success Criteria

| Criteria | What It Looks Like |
|----------|-------------------|
| Complete cycle | Went from planning through harvest (even a small one) |
| Consistent care | Garden was tended most days, not abandoned mid-project |
| Observation skills | Journal has regular entries showing what changed and when |
| Problem-solving | Encountered at least one problem (pests, drought, slow growth) and worked through it |
| Connection to food | Child connects the garden experience to meals and grocery store food |

## Common Pitfalls

- **Over-ambition:** A 20x20-foot garden is a full-time job. Start small. A 4x4 raised bed or 4-5 large pots is plenty for a first garden. Success with a small garden builds confidence for a larger one next year.
- **Inconsistent watering:** This is the #1 garden killer. Build watering into a daily routine — right after breakfast, or right before dinner. Consistency matters more than perfection.
- **Impatience:** The hardest part for children (and adults) is the waiting. Radishes help — they're ready in under a month. But even with fast crops, there are long stretches where "nothing happens" (growth is happening underground or at the cellular level). The journal helps — when you compare Week 1 to Week 3, the change is obvious.
- **Perfectionism:** The garden will not look like a magazine. There will be weeds, yellowed leaves, and imperfect fruit. That's real gardening. Celebrate the imperfect tomato.

## Extensions

- **Seed saving:** At the end of the season, save seeds from your best plants. Dry them, store them, and plant them next year. This is how agriculture worked for 10,000 years.
- **Compost connection:** Connect this project to the Composting 101 activity. Use your own compost to feed the garden.
- **Garden economics:** Track the cost of seeds, soil, and water. Compare to the cost of buying the same produce at the store. (The garden might cost more the first year — that's a real lesson too.)
- **Cook the harvest:** Plan a meal built around what the garden produces. Even if it's just "salad with our lettuce and tomatoes," the pride of eating what you grew is unmatched.

## Safety Notes

### Garden Tools

- Use child-sized tools with appropriate weight — adult shovels and rakes are too heavy and unwieldy for this age group
- Trowels and hand rakes have pointed edges; teach the child to carry them pointed down and never swing or throw them
- Store tools upright or flat after use — a rake left teeth-up is a stepping hazard

### Soil and Biological Hazards

- Always wear garden gloves when handling soil, compost, or mulch — soil can harbor bacteria, fungi, and sharp debris (glass, wire, thorns)
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap after every gardening session, especially before eating
- Do not let children rub their eyes or put fingers in their mouths while working with soil
- If using store-bought compost or fertilizer, the adult handles it — some products contain chemicals not safe for direct skin contact

### Sun and Heat Exposure

- Apply sunscreen before outdoor sessions and reapply if working longer than 30 minutes
- Wear a hat and light-colored clothing on sunny days
- Keep water available for drinking — children dehydrate faster than adults
- Schedule gardening for morning or late afternoon to avoid peak heat; take shade breaks as needed

### Insects and Plants

- Teach children to watch for stinging insects (bees, wasps, fire ants) before reaching into plants or soil
- If the child has a known insect sting allergy, keep an epinephrine auto-injector on hand
- Identify any poisonous plants or mushrooms growing in or near the garden area before the child begins working
- Avoid areas where pesticides or herbicides have been recently applied
